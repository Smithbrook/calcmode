About Compound Interest

Compound interest is the addition of interest to the principal sum of a loan or deposit, so that interest that has been added also earns interest from then on. This compounding effect can significantly increase your investment returns over time.

Albert Einstein allegedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world," noting that "he who understands it, earns it; he who doesn't, pays it." Whether investing for retirement, saving for a major purchase, or planning your financial future, understanding compound interest is crucial for building wealth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?
Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount, while compound interest is calculated on both the principal and accumulated interest. Compound interest grows exponentially over time, making it much more powerful for long-term investing.
How often should interest be compounded?
The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn. Daily compounding is better than monthly, which is better than quarterly or annually. However, the difference becomes smaller as compounding frequency increases.
What's a good interest rate for compound interest?
Historical stock market returns average around 7-10% annually. High-yield savings accounts offer 4-5%, while CDs might offer 3-6%. The "best" rate depends on your risk tolerance and investment timeline.
How does time affect compound interest?
Time is the most powerful factor in compound interest. Starting early, even with smaller amounts, often beats starting late with larger amounts. The exponential growth means the last few years of a long-term investment produce the most dramatic gains.
Can I use this for loans and debt?
Yes! Compound interest also applies to debt. Credit cards and loans often compound interest, which is why debt can grow so quickly. Understanding this helps you prioritize paying off high-interest debt and avoid compound interest working against you.
